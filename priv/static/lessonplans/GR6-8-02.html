Duration: 6 days

GRADE 6, SOCIAL STUDIES

HERITAGE AND IDENTITY

Overview

This lesson will allow students to develop the attributes they require to think critically about the cultures they see and are a part of. Stereotyping and prejudices will be a major focus in this lesson and students will be asked to draw connections between historic communities in Canada and how they have transformed in our current sociey to create PSA videos.

Topics: Aboriginal perspectives, First Nations, minorities, stereotypes, media

Instructional notes

  • This lesson looks at past and present stereotypes and so a great amount of sensitive is necessary when approaching the material. Establishing a strong classroom environment of respect is importantÂ 
  • This lesson includes an interview with an Anishinabe band leader/member which must be arranged prior to the lesson commencing.

Assessment

  • Students will be asked to complete several activities over the course of this lesson that the teacher ca use to assess whether students understanding of the topic is progressing as desired.

Students will be able to:

  • Identify the main reasons why different people came to Canada
  • Identify and describe fundamental elements of Canadian identity
  • Identify various types of communities that have contributed to the development of Canada
  • Describe some key economic, political, cultural, and social aspects of life in settler communities in Canada.

Resources Required

1 Computer/student, access to video cameras (or iPads)

Lesson Plan

Activity & Tools

Tasks

Day 1: Stereotypes of Historical and Contemporary First Nations People

Discussion (class)

Projector and laptop, Worksheet (Appendix I)

Facilitate a discussion around the role of stereotypes in contorting contemporary and historical views of First Nations groups.

Begin by tracing the origins of historical and contemporary stereotypes that dilute many Canadian’s understanding of First Nations communities.

Present images of stereotypical ideas of First Nations People: The Noble Savage, Pocahontas (historical figure vs. appropriations like the Disney animation), etc.

Discuss prejudicial notions that follow contemporary First Nations People. For example: First Nations people are addicts, First Nations people do not pay taxes, etc.

 

Following the discussion, provide a handout asking students whether they agree or disagree with certain claims about First Nations people. See Appendix I for a sample handout.

Activity (pairs)

1 Computer/pair, headphones, Worksheet (Appendix II)

In pairs, students will be assigned an episode to watch from the episodic series 8th Fire which details and destroys misconceptions around the First Nations people. They will be provided with a worksheet that will require them to think about the implications of the ideas represented in the episodes.

Discussion (class)

Gather students back together and have a general discussion of what students learned from the episodes they watched. Provide space on the worksheet for them to add information about the episodes they did not watch.

Day 2: Digital Scavenger Hunt and Interview

Computer work (pairs)

1 Computer/pair, Worksheet (Appendix III)

Introduce students to the concept of primary and secondary research sources.

In pairs, students will find and evaluate primary and secondary sources from the internet to explain what life was historically like for the Anishinabe People. Students are to locate the historical and contemporary location of the band, identify past and present belies/practices/ways of life/industry, as well as issues that are relevant to band members today.

Provide relevant websites (databases) to help guide students.

Interview

Students will take what they have learned from 8th Fire and the digital scavenger hunt to engage in a discussion with a First Nations Band Leader/member from the Anishinabe People.

The communication between the class and the Anishinabe band leader/member can be facilitated through a Skye session if they are unable to visit the school.

Activity (individual)

Students will return to the first activity (Appendix I) to check to see if their initial perceptions were correct and how they may have changed through completing the above activities.

Day 3: Migrants Are All Settlers Activity

Discussion (class)

Students are asked to consider the idea that all migrants to Canada are, in fact, settlers. They are to consider their own cultures’ positions and histories in Canada.

Guide the discussion to the lives of different contemporary communities in Canada and encourage students to make links between their communities (fifth wave migrants) and these or other historical Canadian communities (for example, First Nations, Chinese, Ukrainian).

Prompts: How are these communities similar to each other/ to your community? What are common hardships faced by all historical migrants/visible minorities?

 

Activity (class)

SMART Board

Map some of the cultural backgrounds of students onto an interactive map and facilitate a discussion around why certain populations live n concentrated areas.

Assignment Instruction

Worksheet (Appendix IV)

Introduce students to the Artifacts Box Activity:

Students are to collect artifacts and information from family members that represent their cultures. Students will bring artifacts to the next class and share their stories and histories in small groups. They can detail their own or their ancestors’ migration stories to or throughout Canada.

Sample handout provided in Appendix IV.

Day 4-6

Computer work (groups)

1 Computer/student, access to video cameras (or iPads)

The consolidation activity will stretch over 3 in-class period of instruction (with a final period for screening the finished product).

In the same groups as the Artifacts Box Activity, students will choose one cultural community to collectively demonstrate their understanding of Canadian cultures and attitudes towards them.

Ensure student choices are varied from other groups.

Students will use what they have learned and the materials they have collected to create a short video comparing and contrasting life for historical and contemporary members of the community they choose to research.

Focus one lesson on editing videos in iMovie.
